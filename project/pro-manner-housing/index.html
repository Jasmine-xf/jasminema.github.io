ProManor

Renting in Canada is often shaped by legal uncertainty, fragmented communication, and limited documentation. In a short, exploratory hackathon setting, we examined why many landlords hesitate to rent out their properties and why tenants, especially newcomers, often feel unsure about lease terms and responsibilities. These issues leave both parties entering rental agreements with low confidence and limited trust.

This project explores the lease-making process through a design lens, focusing on how clearer structure, legal guidance, and traceable communication could reduce risk early on. Based on rapid research and prototyping, we proposed an end-to-end lease-making experience that supports informed decision-making and helps landlords and tenants engage with greater clarity from the start.

Background

In cities like Toronto, rental discussions often focus on rising prices and limited housing supply. During early exploration, we noticed a less visible but equally important issue behind this shortage.

Many landlords are reluctant to rent out their properties, not because of low demand, but because the rental process feels risky and unpredictable. Lease responsibilities are unclear, disputes are costly, and evidence is difficult to prepare once problems arise. At the same time, many tenants, especially newcomers to Canada, struggle to understand rental laws and contract terms, which leaves them feeling insecure during the leasing process.

As a result, both parties often enter a lease defensively, with limited trust and transparency from the very beginning.

Project Goal

The goal of ProManor is to improve confidence and clarity at the start of the rental relationship by redesigning the lease-making process itself.

Specifically, we aimed to:

  • Reduce legal and financial uncertainty before a lease is signed
  • Support clear communication and expectation setting between landlords and tenants
  • Preserve reliable records that can be referenced if issues arise later

By improving the process, we hoped to reduce conflict and encourage more housing supply to enter the market.

Desk & Secondary Research

We began by reviewing existing rental platforms, lease templates, and policy documentation related to the Ontario rental market.

This research helped us understand how rental tools currently support, or fail to support, legality, transparency, and evidence creation. Most existing solutions focus on listing and matching, while offering limited support for lease quality, communication records, or post-signing risk management.

These gaps informed the direction of our primary research.

Primary Research

To better understand real-world challenges, we conducted interviews with professional property managers and real estate agents who regularly handle lease agreements and disputes.

Their experience allowed us to identify recurring issues that often escalate into conflicts or legal cases.

Key Findings

Across interviews, four recurring challenges emerged:

  • Credit reports and supporting documents are difficult to verify
  • Custom lease clauses are often written without knowing whether they comply with local laws
  • Late payments and property damage are hard to document in a legally meaningful way
  • Many tenants lack a clear understanding of rental responsibilities and legal boundaries

These findings revealed that many rental problems originate early, long before a formal dispute occurs.

Design Insights

The most important insight was that rental conflicts are rarely caused by bad intent.

Instead, they usually result from unclear expectations, missing documentation, and delayed access to legal guidance. Once a lease is signed, it becomes costly and emotionally draining to correct these issues.

This insight shifted our design focus from enforcement to prevention.

Design Principles

Based on our research, we defined three guiding principles:

  • Reduce uncertainty at the moment of decision
  • Support informed human judgment rather than replace it with automation
  • Ensure actions and communication are traceable over time

These principles guided all major design decisions throughout the project.

Ideation & Concept Exploration

During ideation, we explored multiple ways to support clarity and trust in the rental process, including automated lease creation, centralized communication tools, and evidence-tracking mechanisms.

Rather than treating these as isolated features, we evaluated how each idea fit into the overall lease lifecycle and whether it reduced risk for both parties.

Priority Grid

To scope the project realistically, we evaluated features based on impact and feasibility.

High-priority features included lease clarity tools, communication records, and document verification. More complex ideas such as payment escrow and insurance integration were intentionally deprioritized due to legal complexity and implementation cost.

This prioritization helped us focus on features that could deliver meaningful value within the project timeframe.

To-Be Scenario

In the future experience, landlords and tenants move through a clear, shared lease-making flow.

Landlords can define custom lease requirements while receiving immediate feedback on legality and clarity. Tenants review expectations, submit documents, and communicate questions within the same platform. All key interactions are recorded and accessible, creating a shared understanding before and during the lease.

The goal is not speed, but confidence and predictability.

Wireframing

Based on the To-Be Scenario, we designed wireframes to map out the end-to-end lease-making flow.

The focus at this stage was information structure and task sequencing rather than visual polish. We ensured that users could always see where they were in the process and what actions were required next.

Prototyping

In the high-fidelity prototype, we refined interactions for key moments in the experience.

These included the lease progress tracker, the AI-assisted lease review interface, document upload and verification, and in-platform communication. Special attention was paid to reducing cognitive load when users encountered legal language or complex decisions.

Lean Evaluation

Given time constraints, we focused on early validation rather than extensive testing.

We walked through core user flows with peers and instructors, paying particular attention to clarity, perceived trustworthiness, and ease of understanding. Feedback from these sessions informed small but important adjustments, such as clearer progress indicators and more transparent AI feedback explanations.

Outcome

The final prototype presents a structured and transparent lease-making experience that supports both landlords and tenants.

Rather than replacing human judgment, the system helps users slow down at critical moments, understand their responsibilities, and make decisions they can stand behind later.

Key Takeaways

This project highlighted the importance of designing for clarity in regulated and high-stakes contexts.

Good UX in these environments is not about automation or speed. It is about helping people understand risk, communicate expectations, and create records they can rely on.

Room for Growth

ProManor is an early-stage concept with clear opportunities for further development.

Future work could include a simplified mode for older property owners, stronger guidance around legally admissible evidence, and continued refinement of AI recommendations based on real usage data.
