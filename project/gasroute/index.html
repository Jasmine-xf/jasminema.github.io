Gas Route

Helping drivers stop overthinking gas stops

In Toronto, getting gas is rarely a quick decision. Drivers constantly weigh price differences against detours, traffic, time pressure, and safety. Late at night, that decision gets even heavier.

Most tools make this harder than it needs to be. They show prices and locations, but ignore real context like where someone is headed or whether saving a few cents is actually worth the extra drive. People end up checking multiple options, hesitating, or taking detours they regret later.

Gas Route starts from a simple idea. Refueling is a routine task, and it should not feel like a small math problem every time.

Category
Mobile App Design
Role
UX Designer, Researcher
Duration
3 Weeks

Design Objective

This project is not about finding the absolute cheapest gas.

It is about helping people answer a much more practical question: Where does it make sense to stop right now?

The goal was to reduce unnecessary detours, support safer late night decisions, respect personal habits like fuel type or reward programs, and make repeat comparisons feel lighter instead of exhausting.

After talking to users and reviewing survey responses, a few patterns showed up again and again.

  • People cared about price, but only when the savings felt meaningful.
  • At night, safety often became the deciding factor.
  • Many drivers trusted familiar stations or places tied to reward programs.
  • Most importantly, people did not feel confused by a lack of data. They felt unsure about how to choose.

That insight changed the direction of the project. The problem was not missing information. It was missing confidence.

Instead of asking how to show cheaper gas, we asked how to help someone quickly decide whether a specific station was worth going to at that moment, given their route, time, and priorities.

From there, Gas Route became less about comparison and more about decision support.

Persona as Design Lens

To keep decisions grounded, I created a core persona based on research patterns.

Gastina is a 28 year old driver in the GTA who often gets gas after work. She wants to save money, avoid wasting time, and feel safe. Still, she regularly wonders whether driving out of the way for cheaper gas was actually worth it.

Rather than treating the persona as a profile, I used it as a checkpoint. Does this design help Gastina decide faster and with less doubt?

Key Need Statements

Need 1: Accurate Information

Users need clear and reliable gas station information so they can trust prices and basic details before deciding where to stop.

Solution: Gas Route presents up-to-date pricing and station details in a clear, scannable format, helping users confirm key information before committing to a stop.

Need 2: Lower Price Without Detours

Users want to save money on gas, but not at the cost of extra driving time, fuel waste, or unnecessary route changes.

Solution: Gas Route recommends stations along or near the user's current route, balancing price benefits with distance to avoid inefficient detours.

Need 3: Safety Awareness

Users need a quick way to judge whether a gas station feels safe, especially at night or in unfamiliar areas.

Solution: Gas Route offers lightweight safety cues that allow users to assess surroundings at a glance before deciding to refuel.

Need 4: Time Efficiency

Users want refueling to fit naturally into their routine without adding friction or disrupting their commute.

Solution: Gas Route reduces steps between decision and action, enabling fast navigation and smoother integration into daily travel.

To prioritize potential solutions, we evaluated each idea through two lenses: impact and feasibility.

Impact focused on how effectively a solution could address core user needs and improve the overall refueling experience. Feasibility considered how realistically the idea could be implemented within project constraints, without sacrificing clarity or quality.

Gas Route supports refueling decisions through a few simple ideas.

  • Users set basic preferences during onboarding.
  • Recommendations respond to route context instead of price alone.
  • Quick safety cues and one tap navigation help users act right away, especially at night.

Together, these choices shorten the distance between thinking and driving.

Low Fidelity Design and Validation

From Sketches to Low-Fi Flows

We began with sketches and low-fidelity flows to map out the experience before focusing on visual design. At this stage, the goal was to understand how users move through the app and make decisions while refueling.

We designed three low-fi flows, each covering a key part of the journey:

Account setup and preferences, where users define fuel type, price range, and loyalty programs

Searching for gas stations, using filters, station details, and optional real-time camera views for safety

Selecting from recommendations or favorites, then handing off to navigation

These flows helped us connect setup, comparison, and action into a single experience.

Insights from Testing

We tested the low-fi flows through usability testing and in-class presentations, focusing on clarity and task completion without additional explanation.

Several issues surfaced:

  • Transitions between search results and station details were not always clear
  • Price, distance, and safety information competed for attention
  • Some actions felt repetitive across screens

The feedback confirmed the concept, but showed where the flow needed to be more focused.

Refinement into Mid-Fidelity Wireframes

Based on testing feedback, we refined the flows into mid-fidelity wireframes. The focus shifted to clarity and consistency rather than visual polish.

In the mid-fi version, we simplified task steps, clarified decision points, and reorganized information hierarchy. Actions such as selecting a station, checking safety, and starting navigation became more direct and consistent.

These changes reduced friction and better reflected how users make real-world refueling decisions.

Reflection

This project reinforced that for everyday tasks, reducing mental effort often matters more than adding features.

It also showed that research becomes most valuable when it changes how decisions are supported, not just how screens look.

Low fidelity design turned out to be an effective way to think through ideas and test assumptions early.

Opportunities for Growth

Future work could test recommendations with a larger group, explore even lighter safety signals, and fine tune alert timing so it stays helpful without becoming distracting.
