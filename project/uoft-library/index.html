UofT Library Navigation

Navigating the University of Toronto’s St. George campus is often challenging due to complex building layouts, inconsistent signage, and limited accessibility support. Existing tools such as Google Maps or static floor plans cannot provide accurate, room-level guidance, leaving many students, visitors, and individuals with accessibility needs struggling to find their way efficiently.

This project investigates the barriers to campus wayfinding and defines the high-priority requirements for an indoor navigation system tailored to UofT. Based on surveys, interviews, and thematic analysis, we identified six core needs — real-time positioning, accessible routes, room-level search, clear landmarks, multilingual support, and an intuitive campus map — leading to a proposed solution that reduces navigation time, improves accessibility, and enhances the overall campus experience.

Desk Research

To understand how people navigate complex indoor spaces and what challenges they face, our team conducted desk research as part of collaborative project with Mobile Application Development Lab (MADLab) at the University of Toronto

The goal was to determine what practices work well, what challenges remain unsolved, and how these insights could inform the design direction for UofT's indoor navigation system.

How We do that?

  • Reviewed 10+ reports and navigation tools to examine common usability and accessibility issues.
  • Compared how different systems approach real-time positioning, visual cues, and accessibility features.
  • Mapped out key opportunities that informed our primary research plan and guided later design decisions.

Key Findings

  • 3D/AR navigation and real-time tracking are industry standards, but few solutions integrate accessibility features such as screen readers, high-contrast modes, or barrier-free routes.
    → There's a gap in inclusive indoor navigation design.
  • Information about indoor spaces is fragmented and static. Most tools rely on outdated floor maps or scattered signage, which causes confusion and inconsistency across buildings.
    → There's a need for a centralized, real-time navigation system that updates continuously.
  • User confidence and independence are limited. Research shows that many people—especially newcomers and those with accessibility needs—depend on asking others for directions.
    → There's an opportunity to build an experience that supports independent navigation through clearer visual and contextual cues.
  • Offline and multi-language functions are underdeveloped. Few systems support use without internet access or offer multilingual interfaces.
    → There's potential for offline usability and language inclusivity in UofT's campus environment.

Primary Research

To understand the existing landscape of indoor navigation and identify gaps in usability and accessibility, our team conducted secondary research as part of our collaboration with MADLab. We aimed to learn how other systems handle complex spatial navigation, what features users value most, and where current solutions fall short — especially in supporting accessibility and user independence on campus.

How We do that?

Survey

Identify broad indoor navigation challenges, preferred features, and accessibility needs

How We Conducted the Survey

  • Built in Google Forms
  • Structure: informed consent + screener + survey
  • Distributed via word-of-mouth, group chats, forums
  • Collected 50 valid responses from students and recent alumni

Interview

Identify in-depth user needs, pain points, and expectations in campus wayfinding.

How We Conducted the Interview

  • Recruited participants through word-of-mouth and on-site outreach in UofT buildings
  • Each session lasted ~30 minutes (screening, consent, 8 open-ended questions)
  • Audio-recorded and transcribed for thematic analysis
  • Conducted 10 interviews with current UofT students

Result Analysis

After analyzing the survey and interview results, we identified three key directions that guided our design decisions:

Participants described confusing signages, inconsistent room numbering, and complex building layouts that made wayfinding difficult.

Many relied on trial and error or asking others for help, showing a lack of independence in navigation.

Users expressed a strong desire for more visual indicators and believed that digital tools could make navigation easier and more intuitive.

Persona

We created Emily, a UofT psychology student who frequently moves between buildings and often feels lost due to confusing layouts.
By synthesizing survey and interview insights, we built this persona to capture users' real frustrations and guide our design toward improving confidence and independent navigation.

Design Concept Exploration

Ideation

We brainstormed creative ways to solve key navigation issues—confusing layouts, inconsistent signage, and limited accessibility support. Using “How Might We” questions, our team generated and sketched multiple ideas, such as a 360° view, predictive search, positioning, and accessibility filters. We then discussed feasibility and selected the strongest concepts to move forward into the Priority Grid phase.

Priority Grid

To evaluate our ideas, we used a Priority Grid to balance impact and feasibility

Each team member voted independently, then discussed results to identify which ideas would bring the most value within project constraints.

Through this process, we prioritized six core features — 360° navigation, real-time positioning, accessibility filters, predictive search, minimap, and clear landmarks — forming the foundation of our high-fidelity prototype.

We prioritized ideas based on impact and feasibility. Our final focus areas include:

  • Home Runs: Step-by-step guidance, search bar, accessibility toggle, filters, and favorites
  • Big Bets: Real-time GPS and AR navigation glasses (for future exploration)
  • Quick Wins: QR code scanning and manual location input
  • Marginal Gains: VR headset exploration (low priority)

To-Be Scenario

Building on our research, ideation, and the Priority Grid, the future experience enables students to navigate independently—quickly locating rooms, understanding where they are, and choosing accessible routes without asking for help or relying on live Wi-Fi.

We prioritized ideas based on impact and feasibility. Our final focus areas include:

  • Start & Locate: From the home screen, users either search a room or scan/enter a marker to set their current location. A "You are here" indicator confirms orientation.
  • Preview & Decide: A minimap with floor selector shows the shortest path; 360° indoor view provides spatial context. Users can apply filters (e.g., washrooms, elevators) or toggle Accessibility to avoid stairs.
  • Navigate Flexibly: Step-by-step thumbnails and clear visual cues guide movement; users can skip steps or open a full-route overview at any time.
  • Learn In-Flow: Tapping pins reveals concise room/service details (hours, availability) without leaving the page.
  • Return & Reuse: Recent and saved routes allow one-click renavigation to frequent destinations.

Waireframe

Building on our research insights and To-Be Scenario, we designed wireframes to visualize a clear, accessible navigation flow.

Since users may need to navigate in real time while holding their phones, we designed a mobile version in addition to the web version. Although both share the same overall design concept, the mobile version places greater emphasis on gesture interactions and aligns more closely with users' mobile usage habits.

Lean Evaluation

To quickly validate the core “find–view–locate–navigate” user flow at low cost before moving into high-fi prototyping.

Why

After completing our low-fidelity web and mobile wireframes, we conducted a lean evaluation to validate early navigation flows and identify usability gaps before building high-fidelity prototypes. This step helped us ensure that our design addressed key user goals such as clarity, accessibility, and independence during wayfinding.

How

We ran 4 quick usability sessions with UofT students in MADLab's early testing environment.

  • Materials: printed and digital wireframes for both desktop and mobile versions.
  • Setting: conducted on campus (Gerstein Library, Bahen Centre) through convenience sampling.
  • Method: participants completed basic navigation tasks (searching rooms, switching floors, closing pop-ups, and using filters) while thinking aloud.
  • Observation Focus: interaction clarity, layout consistency, and accessibility readiness (contrast, font size, icons).

Key Insights

  • Users instinctively tried to close pop-ups by tapping outside the box, not just the "X" button.
  • The bottom navigation displayed too many steps; users preferred seeing only 2–3 at once.
  • The minimap lacked readability—participants asked for stronger color contrast and clearer landmarks.
  • Accessibility elements such as text size and contrast needed improvement for real-world usability.
  • Filters were well-received but required clearer icons for washrooms, exits, and study zones.

These findings informed immediate refinements to our wireframes and shaped the foundation for our high-fidelity prototype, ensuring better navigation flow and accessibility alignment with MADLab's design goals.

Prototyping

Based on our lean evaluation and usability testing, we made several targeted refinements to improve clarity, accessibility, and navigation flow. Our final prototype focused on creating a smoother and more intuitive experience: users can now close pop-ups by tapping outside the box, view only 2–3 navigation steps at once, explore a higher-contrast and expandable minimap, enjoy larger and clearer icons and text, receive a start-point reminder before navigation begins, and switch between navigation steps more flexibly.

Mobile Interaction Flow — Detailed Walkthrough

Step 1 · Search Room

Users tap the top search bar and type a room number or name. Predictive suggestions appear in real time, allowing users to quickly select a destination.

Step 2 · Review Room Info

A concise information card appears with photos, description, opening hours, accessibility tags, and a "Step-by-Step" toggle.

Step 3 · Start-Point Reminder

Before starting navigation, a small pop-up reminds users to begin from a specific entrance.

Step 4 · Indoor View (360°)

The immersive view allows users to look around by swiping and follow directional arrows along the path. A large "Exit" button ensures users can return anytime.

Step 5 · Choose Floor

A vertical floor selector enables quick switching between building levels while keeping the minimap synced.

Step 6 · View / Expand Minimap

Users can toggle between half and full minimap modes to see route details or focus on the 360° view.

Step 7 · View Saved Routes

The "Saved Routes" tab lets users revisit frequent destinations with one tap.

Desktop Interaction Flow — Detailed Walkthrough

Step 1 · Home Page

Users land on the main page featuring a universal search bar, accessibility filters (eg, washrooms, elevators), and a 360° indoor preview. The right side displays a minimap with a floor selector and navigation overview.

Step 2 · Search Destination

Users type their destination in the search bar; predictive results appear instantly. Once selected, the minimap and 360° indoor view update in sync.

Step 3 · Room Details & Hotspots

Clicking any hotspot or result opens an inline info panel.

Step 4 · Step-by-Step Directions

Navigation steps appear as thumbnail previews below the 360° view; users can click arrows or thumbnails to move forward or skip steps.

Step 5 · Minimap & Floor Selector

The minimap can expand or collapse, showing real-time paths and labeled landmarks. Floor levels can be switched via a vertical selector, keeping both the 360° view and map aligned.

Step 6 · Accessibility & Independence

Users can toggle accessibility filters to view barrier-free routes and activate screen-reader compatibility.

Outcome

The desktop version complements the mobile flow by supporting pre-trip planning and spatial learning. Together, they enable users to search, preview, and navigate UofT's complex indoor spaces independently — both online and offline.

Key Takeaways

  • Early lean evaluation helped us identify usability issues quickly and refine our wireframes before committing to high-fidelity design.
  • Testing both desktop and mobile prototypes proved essential—each revealed unique navigation needs in different use contexts.
  • Clear visual hierarchy and contrast significantly improved accessibility and reduced user confusion during navigation tasks.
  • Users valued independent wayfinding most; minimizing reliance on others became the central goal of our interaction design.
  • Continuous iteration—rooted in user feedback and inclusive design principles —ensured our final prototype was both intuitive and accessible for diverse users.

Room for Growth

  • Conduct additional usability tests with a broader participant group to validate design scalability across various campus buildings.
  • Explore more creative path guidance alternatives (eg, color-coded routes, light cues) that meet client constraints while maintaining clarity.
  • Integrate manual location teaching into onboarding to help users position themselves without GPS.
  • Expand accessibility support—add adjustable text size, keyboard navigation, and multilingual features for future iterations.
