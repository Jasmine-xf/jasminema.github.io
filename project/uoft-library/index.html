UofT Library Navigation

Navigating the University of Toronto’s St. George campus is often challenging due to complex building layouts, inconsistent signage, and limited accessibility support. Existing tools such as Google Maps or static floor plans cannot provide accurate, room-level guidance, leaving many students, visitors, and individuals with accessibility needs struggling to find their way efficiently.

This project investigates the barriers to campus wayfinding and defines the high-priority requirements for an indoor navigation system tailored to UofT. Based on surveys, interviews, and thematic analysis, we identified six core needs — real-time positioning, accessible routes, room-level search, clear landmarks, multilingual support, and an intuitive campus map — leading to a proposed solution that reduces navigation time, improves accessibility, and enhances the overall campus experience.

Desk Research

To understand how people navigate complex indoor spaces and what challenges they face, our team conducted desk research as part of collaborative project with Mobile Application Development Lab (MADLab) at the University of Toronto

The goal was to determine what practices work well, what challenges remain unsolved, and how these insights could inform the design direction for UofT's indoor navigation system.

How We do that?

  • Reviewed 10+ reports and navigation tools to examine common usability and accessibility issues.
  • Compared how different systems approach real-time positioning, visual cues, and accessibility features.
  • Mapped out key opportunities that informed our primary research plan and guided later design decisions.

Key Findings

  • 3D/AR navigation and real-time tracking are industry standards, but few solutions integrate accessibility features such as screen readers, high-contrast modes, or barrier-free routes.
    → There's a gap in inclusive indoor navigation design.
  • Information about indoor spaces is fragmented and static. Most tools rely on outdated floor maps or scattered signage, which causes confusion and inconsistency across buildings.
    → There's a need for a centralized, real-time navigation system that updates continuously.
  • User confidence and independence are limited. Research shows that many people—especially newcomers and those with accessibility needs—depend on asking others for directions.
    → There's an opportunity to build an experience that supports independent navigation through clearer visual and contextual cues.
  • Offline and multi-language functions are underdeveloped. Few systems support use without internet access or offer multilingual interfaces.
    → There's potential for offline usability and language inclusivity in UofT's campus environment.

Primary Research

To understand the existing landscape of indoor navigation and identify gaps in usability and accessibility, our team conducted secondary research as part of our collaboration with MADLab. We aimed to learn how other systems handle complex spatial navigation, what features users value most, and where current solutions fall short — especially in supporting accessibility and user independence on campus.

How We do that?

Survey

Identify broad indoor navigation challenges, preferred features, and accessibility needs

How We Conducted the Survey

  • Built in Google Forms
  • Structure: informed consent + screener + survey
  • Distributed via word-of-mouth, group chats, forums
  • Collected 50 valid responses from students and recent alumni

Interview

Identify in-depth user needs, pain points, and expectations in campus wayfinding.

How We Conducted the Interview

  • Recruited participants through word-of-mouth and on-site outreach in UofT buildings
  • Each session lasted ~30 minutes (screening, consent, 8 open-ended questions)
  • Audio-recorded and transcribed for thematic analysis
  • Conducted 10 interviews with current UofT students

Key Findings

Participants described confusing signages, inconsistent room numbering, and complex building layouts that made wayfinding difficult.

Many relied on trial and error or asking others for help, showing a lack of independence in navigation.

Users expressed a strong desire for more visual indicators and believed that digital tools could make navigation easier and more intuitive.

Result Analysis

Together, these findings revealed that users need clearer guidance, real-time feedback, and digital support to navigate indoor spaces effectively. This led us to define four ideal solutions addressing both functional and accessibility needs:

  • Search feature to navigate specific indoor locations
  • Real-time GPS / QR code to locate the exact level and room
  • Floor plan implementation for each level
  • Augmented Reality (AR) navigation for enhanced spatial awareness (*pending technical feasibility)

User Interface Design

I designed a mobile-first interface that prioritizes clarity and ease of use. The interface provides clear visual hierarchy, intuitive navigation controls, and contextual information. The design accommodates different user types and navigation scenarios while maintaining consistency across library buildings.

05. Lean Evaluation

Why?

Lean evaluation is the first step in the iterative usability tests for continuous UX refinement. It not only lays the groundwork for our prototype, but also helps to identify major design flaws early on.

How?

  • Conducted 10 guerrilla usability tests by approaching students with our wireframes on paper cards.
  • Convenience sampling: UofT campus / library locations
  • Used incentives such as coffee vouchers for their time in exchange for participation.
  • Employed the same screening and consent protocol as our interviews and surveys.
  • Each paper card represents a clickable action, presented in sequence to participants.
  • Observation was in effect to see how participants interacted with our wireframe.
  • Think aloud protocol was used to encourage speak aloud thought processes.

Wireframes

Step 1: Search Location

Step 2: View Directions

Step 3: Navigate Route

Key Insights

  • Users struggled with complex navigation flows and couldn't easily backtrack when they made mistakes.
  • Participants couldn't skip unnecessary onboarding steps to reach the main functionality faster.
  • Users couldn't select multiple location types simultaneously when filtering search results.
  • After finding suitable locations, users lacked in-app registration or bookmarking options.
  • During navigation, users felt lost and disoriented without clear progress indicators.

Key Takeaways

  1. Designing Survey/Interview Questions: Ensuring vocabularies and expressions are non-leading and do not make assumptions about user behavior.
  2. Interviews and Rapport: Building rapport and trust, practicing active listening during interviews to produce accurate, unbiased responses.
  3. Mixed Research Methodology: Employing a mixed research methodology is beneficial when a project requires both quantitative and qualitative data.
  4. Lean Evaluation: Lean evaluation as a method for iterative refinement of design solutions, aiming for continuous improvement to meet ever-evolving user needs.
  5. User Navigation: Providing users with multiple means of navigating freely within a prototype, specifically the freedom to back up and switch between different navigation modes.

Usability Testing

The UofT Library Navigation project taught me that designing for indoor wayfinding requires a deep understanding of spatial relationships and user behavior in physical environments. Working with complex building layouts showed how important it is to provide clear visual guidance and break down navigation into manageable steps. I also learned how critical real-time data integration is for building user trust and ensuring system accuracy. Most importantly, this project reinforced the value of user research in understanding diverse navigation needs and the importance of designing for different user types and scenarios.
