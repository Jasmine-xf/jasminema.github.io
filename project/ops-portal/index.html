Public-Sector Web Registry System

The Public-Sector Web Registry System is an internal tool used across a large government organization to manage website records, domains, and governance data. The system supports hundreds of program teams who rely on accurate website information for audits, renewals, compliance, and day-to-day content management.

Uerstanding the Challenge

The Challenge

Government teams rely on accurate website and domain information to maintain service quality.

But the previous system was:

  • Difficult to search
  • Hard to navigate
  • Inconsistent in layout
  • Missing clear guidance for completing key tasks

Users were taking far too long to locate simple information such as website owners, URLs, statuses, or expiry dates. New users needed extensive training, and even experienced web stewards often struggled with exporting data or adding records without errors.

Design Decisions

To make the Citizen Service Portal not only visually appealing but also accessible and inclusive, I established a set of design decisions and content guidelines. These were informed by both primary research (user interviews & surveys) and secondary research (best practices & accessibility standards).

Accessibility-first design: Every component and interaction pattern is designed with WCAG 2.1 AA compliance as the baseline, ensuring inclusive access for all citizens.

Task-based organization: Services are organized by what citizens want to accomplish rather than which department provides them, reducing cognitive load and improving findability.

Progressive disclosure: Complex multi-step processes are broken down into manageable steps with clear progress indicators and contextual help.

Consistent interaction patterns: Unified design system ensures consistent behavior across all government services, building user confidence and reducing learning curve.

Multiple support channels: Integration of phone, chat, and in-person assistance options to support users with different comfort levels and needs.

Data-driven optimization: Analytics and user feedback loops inform continuous improvement and ensure the portal evolves with citizen needs.

Accessibility

WCAG 2.1 AA Compliance

For this project, I ensured accessibility by implementing full WCAG 2.1 AA compliance across all components and interactions. This includes semantic HTML, proper ARIA labels, keyboard navigation, and screen reader optimization.

  1. Conducted comprehensive accessibility audits using automated tools and manual testing with assistive technologies.
  2. Implemented semantic HTML structure with proper heading hierarchy and landmark regions for screen reader navigation.
  3. Designed keyboard-accessible interactions with visible focus indicators and logical tab order throughout the interface.

Inclusive Design Principles

I applied inclusive design principles to ensure the portal serves citizens with diverse abilities and needs. This includes clear visual hierarchy, readable typography, and multiple ways to access information. The design accommodates users with different technical comfort levels and provides appropriate support mechanisms.

Reflections

The Citizen Service Portal project taught me that designing for government services requires a deep understanding of accessibility, inclusivity, and the diverse needs of citizens. Working with complex organizational structures showed how important it is to prioritize user needs over internal processes. I also learned how critical it is to design with accessibility from the start rather than retrofitting it later. Most importantly, this project reinforced the value of inclusive design and the responsibility we have as designers to ensure digital services are accessible to everyone in our communities.
